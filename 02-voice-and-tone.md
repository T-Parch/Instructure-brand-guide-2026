---

## The Distinction
**Voice** = consistent personality (never changes)
**Tone** = situational mood (adapts to context and audience)

---

## Voice: The Three Constants

### Clear
Favor straightforward language over technical terminology. Make complex subjects accessible. If a sentence needs a glossary to parse, rewrite it.

### Confident
Demonstrate genuine expertise — not arrogance. Assured language reflects real knowledge and quality products. Don't hedge unnecessarily.

### Conversational
Write like a smart, helpful human. Use contractions. Reference relatable examples. Humor and genuine understanding are welcome. Avoid corporate stiffness.

---

## Tone: The Five Variables

| Tone Quality | What It Sounds Like | What to Avoid |
|-------------|-------------------|---------------|
| **Knowledgeable** | Demonstrates expertise, cites specifics | Know-it-all, pedantic |
| **Encouraging** | Motivates without judgment, supports risk-taking | Patronizing, hand-holding |
| **Sincere** | Authentic understanding of educator needs | Saccharine, over-the-top positivity |
| **Playful** | Cultural references, wordplay, relaxed | Juvenile, unprofessional |
| **Professional** | Clear, direct, conversational | Stuffy, robotic |

---

## Quick application guide

**For product marketing copy:** Lead with Knowledgeable + Encouraging. The audience knows their job — meet them there.

**For social/email:** Lean Playful + Sincere. Real humans, real moments.

**For legal or compliance content:** Professional + Sincere. Clear and trustworthy, not cold.

**For customer success / support-adjacent content:** Encouraging + Knowledgeable. Supportive, not patronizing.

**For APAC/EMEA audiences:** Same voice, same tone — adjust spelling conventions per glossary.

---

## What brand voice is NOT
- AI-generated filler
- Corporate buzzwords ("leverage," "empower," "unlock")
- Passive, process-focused writing where software is the protagonist
- Excessive exclamation points as a substitute for genuine warmth
