# Case study template — Instructure
**Version:** v1.1.0
**Last verified:** 2026-05-18
**Based on:** Seven reference case studies across Canvas Studio (Excelsior, NWFSC), Canvas Career (Idaho State), Mastery (Lexington), LearnPlatform (New Hanover), and Parchment (United ISD, VLACS).
**Use with:** Files 01–17 of the Instructure Brand Guide bundle. This file does not replace voice, style, or product messaging rules — it applies them to one specific asset type.

---

## Why this file exists

Every Instructure case study follows the same shape — same four-act narrative, same two-page layout, same set of design moves — with the product color, logo, and URL swapped in. This file captures that shape so a Claude project can draft new case studies that drop into the existing design template without rework.

This file covers four things:
1. The narrative structure (Situation → Insights → Solution → Outcomes).
2. The page-by-page layout, so writers know what they're writing into.
3. The brand-specific variations (Canvas, Mastery, Parchment, LearnPlatform, etc.) so the right colors, logos, and URLs get used.
4. The acceptable structural variants — places where the template flexes without breaking.

---

## The four-act narrative

Every case study is built on the same four sections, in this order. Don't rename them, don't reorder them, don't combine them.

| Section | Job to be done | Length |
|---------|---------------|--------|
| **Situation** (or "The situation") | Set the scene. Who is the customer, what was happening, what wasn't working. | 80–180 words |
| **Insights** | Show the moment the customer realized the deeper problem. The turning point — surface the underlying need, not just the surface symptom. | 70–150 words |
| **Solution** | Describe what they implemented and how the rollout went. Concrete actions, not adjectives. | 80–180 words |
| **Outcomes** | Prove it worked. Lead with the numbers, follow with the human moments. | 80–200 words |

### Section header conventions

Both "Situation" and "The situation" are in use across the live library. Sentence case is the rule — pick one and stay consistent within a single case study. The shorter forms ("Situation," "Insights," "Solution," "Outcomes") read slightly cleaner; the longer "The situation" feels a touch more narrative. Either works.

### Section-by-section writing guidance

**Situation.** Open with a specific, attention-getting fact (a number, a contradiction, a vivid detail). Name the customer, the size, the location. Describe what they were doing and where it broke. Don't editorialize — let the facts carry the weight.

> Strong opener (Lexington): *"Just because a district is small doesn't mean the state assessment and student achievement stakes are low."*
>
> Strong opener (United ISD): *"15,000 physical transcripts a year can really bog down a team."*
>
> Strong opener (VLACS): *"As one of the largest statewide virtual schools in the country, Virtual Learning Academy Charter School (VLACS) offers flexibility outside of the traditional model."*

**Optional "Challenges:" bulleted subsection.** Three of the seven references (New Hanover, UISD, VLACS) include a short bulleted "Challenges:" block inside or alongside the Situation. It works well when the customer faces three or more distinct pain points worth itemizing. Format:

```
Challenges:
- [Specific challenge with concrete detail]
- [Specific challenge with concrete detail]
- [Specific challenge with concrete detail]
```

Use this variant when the problems are parallel and discrete. Skip it when the situation is one connected story.

**Insights.** This is the "aha" beat. What did the customer realize once they looked closer? It's rarely the obvious problem — it's the underlying friction, the broken assumption, or the new requirement they couldn't keep up with. End this section pointing toward Instructure as the path forward, without yet describing the solution.

**Solution.** What did they actually do? Name the product, name the steps, name the people. Bulleted lists are welcome here when the rollout has clear phases. Avoid vague claims like "the team partnered with Instructure" — say what happened.

**Outcomes.** Lead with the strongest stat or shift, then build the picture. Bulleted lists work well for results. End with a forward-looking line if there's a natural one — what's next for the customer, or what the partnership unlocks. The closing should feel earned, not promotional.

- Down-arrow icon in product color
- CTA line + product URL in product color (formatted as a link)

**Quote count flex.** Two quotes total is the most common pattern (one per page). Each additional voice should earn its place by adding a distinct perspective.

## Voice and tone for case studies

Apply file 02 (voice and tone). For case studies specifically:

**Lead with:** Knowledgeable + Sincere. The customer is the hero — Instructure is the partner.

**Avoid:** Hyperbole, vendor-speak, and any framing where the software is the protagonist. Sentences like "Canvas transformed teaching" are off-brand. Sentences like "Canvas Studio gave teachers visibility into how students engaged with video" are on-brand.

**Quote rules:**
- Quotes should sound like a real human said them. If a quote reads like marketing wrote it, cut it.
- Attribute every quote with name, title, and organization (italic, smaller than the quote).
- Use em dash + space before attribution: *"Quote." — Name, Title*
- Page 1 quote tends to be a strategic statement from a senior leader.
- Page 2 quotes tend to be more specific — a number, a moment, a hands-on observation.

**Banned in case studies specifically** (in addition to the file 04 ban list):
- "revolutionize," "transform," "unlock," "empower," "harness," "leverage"
- "industry-leading," "best-in-class," "cutting-edge"
- "the future of [anything]"
- Vague phrases like "a true partner" or "amazing results" without the specifics that earn them

## Stats block guidance

Two or three stats only. Pick the ones that frame the story before the reader reads a word.

**Good stat categories:**
- Adoption year (e.g., "Adopted Canvas Studio: 2025")
- Scale (e.g., "Students: 2,600" or "Full-time enrolled students: 5,000")
- Volume of change (e.g., "Videos Migrated: 40,000+")
- Headline result (e.g., "Turnaround time: 2 weeks → 2 minutes")

**Grouped stats variation.** When two stats are related, use a single group header above both. Idaho State pairs *"Annual program enrollment"* as a header above *"Workforce training: 13,000+"* and *"K–12 professional development: 38,000+"*. This is cleaner than three standalone stats when the numbers belong together.

**Format:**
- Label first, smaller weight: *"Videos Migrated"*
- Numeral or short phrase second, large weight: *"40,000+"*
- Numerals always, never spelled out
- No decimals unless the precision matters
- Use a plus sign (`+`) when the number is approximate or growing
- Use an en dash for ranges: *"K–12"*

## Pull quote guidance

Two to three quotes total per case study.

**Page 1 quote.** A foundational statement about the work or partnership. Often from the senior leader (director, chief officer, superintendent). One quote.

**Page 2 quote(s).** A moment of impact or insight. Often longer, more specific, more human. One or two quotes — when using two, vary the speaker for a multi-voice perspective (NWFSC pattern).

Format:
```
┃ [Quote in larger type, regular weight, no italics]
┃
┃ — Name
┃   Title, Organization (italic, smaller)
```

The vertical bar is the product color.

## Most common brand misses to watch

These are the issues that show up in case study drafts more than anywhere else. Run through this list before handing off.

1. **"edtech" capitalized in a title.** The case study title uses Title Case (it's a published work), but "edtech" stays lowercase even in title case per the glossary. Watch for this — one of the reference case studies has "Edtech" in a title and it's a miss.
2. **Possessive product names.** "Canvas's gradebook" is wrong. "The Canvas gradebook" is right. Same for "Mastery's reports" → "Mastery reports" or "the Mastery reports."
3. **"Instructure Canvas."** Never say this. Instructure is the parent company, not a modifier. Use "Canvas" alone after the first mention, or "Canvas by Instructure" for the first mention.
4. **Product color used for headlines or body copy.** Never. Headlines on Navy use Sky. Headlines on white use Navy. Product color is decorative only.
5. **Missing "By Instructure" byline.** Every product logo must include it. If you're handing copy to a designer, flag it in the file notes.
6. **Pull quote attribution formatting.** Em dash + name, title in italic underneath. Don't put a period after the title.
7. **Stat block with more than three stats.** Three is the max. Four feels crowded and dilutes the strongest number.
8. **Closing line that promotes Instructure.** Case studies close on the customer, not the company. End on what's next for them or what the partnership unlocks for their students.
9. **Banned vocabulary creeping in.** Re-scan for "transform," "unlock," "empower," "leverage," "revolutionize." Replace with verbs that name a specific action.
10. **Oxford commas missing in lists.** "Canvas, Mastery, and Parchment" — always.
11. **Mixing section header styles.** Stay consistent within a single case study — all "The situation"/"The insights" or all "Situation"/"Insights." Don't mix.

## Quality checklist before handoff

Run this before sending to design or brand review.

- [ ] Four sections present and in order: Situation, Insights, Solution, Outcomes
- [ ] Section headers are sentence case and consistent throughout (all short form or all "The" prefixed)
- [ ] Case study title is title case, and "edtech" (if present) is lowercase
- [ ] Customer name, size, and location appear in the first three sentences
- [ ] At least one quote per page, attributed with name and title
- [ ] Two or three stats in the stat block (group header optional when stats are related)
- [ ] No possessive product names
- [ ] "By Instructure" included with first product mention in body copy
- [ ] No "Instructure Canvas" or other parent-as-modifier constructions
- [ ] Banned vocabulary scrubbed (transform, unlock, empower, leverage, harness, revolutionize, robust, seamless, future-of)
- [ ] Oxford commas present in all serial lists
- [ ] Em dashes set without spaces (per style guide)
- [ ] En dashes used for ranges (K–12, 2024–2025)
- [ ] Numerals used for stats and dates
- [ ] Customer is the hero in the closing paragraph
- [ ] CTA matches the product URL
- [ ] Design notes call out: product logo with byline, product color for accents only, hero photo style, decorative circles, footer URL, QR code if applicable

## Translating design into project context

A Claude project can't render layouts, but it can produce a writer's brief and a designer's brief together — so the design team has everything they need to drop the copy into the template. When asking Claude to draft a case study, the output should include three things:

1. **The copy** — title, subhead, four sections, stats, quotes, CTA. Ready for design.
2. **The design notes** — product logo to use, primary accent color and hex, hero photo direction, secondary photo or screenshot direction, decorative circle placement, footer URL, QR code if applicable. Brand-correct by default.
3. **The brand QA note** — strictness level used, any flags (missing proof points, claims that need legal review, photos that need to be shot or sourced).

When the project is used for review instead of drafting, output the same three things — with diffs or annotations on the copy and a fresh design notes block if anything needs updating.
